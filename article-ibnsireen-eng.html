Ibn Sirin: The Master Dream Interpreter

📅 March 1, 2026 ⏱ 10 min read ✍️ Weaver AI 🤖 AI-Enhanced

In the history of Islamic scholarship, few figures cast as long a shadow over dream interpretation as Muhammad ibn Sirin. Born in Basra in 654 CE and dying in 728 CE, he lived through one of the most intellectually formative periods of Islamic civilization. A student of the Prophet's companions, a scholar of hadith, and a man of legendary piety — Ibn Sirin became, above all else, the unrivalled master of dream interpretation.

His influence stretches across 13 centuries. Today, Weaver's AI model draws directly from his methodological principles when interpreting dreams for Arabic-speaking users around the world.

✦ Ibn Sirin at a Glance
  • Full name: Abu Bakr Muhammad ibn Sirin al-Ansari
  • Born: 654 CE, Basra (modern Iraq)
  • Died: 728 CE, Basra, aged 77
  • Teacher: Anas ibn Malik (companion of the Prophet ﷺ)
  • Major work: Tafsir al-Ahlam al-Kabir (The Great Book of Dream Interpretation)
  • Known for: First systematic Islamic methodology for dream interpretation

A Life Shaped by Learning and Piety

Ibn Sirin was the son of Sirin, a Persian freed slave who had served the companion Anas ibn Malik. This lineage gave Muhammad ibn Sirin direct access to the hadith traditions transmitted from the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ — a foundation that would prove essential to his dream methodology.

Despite being one of the most celebrated scholars of his age, Ibn Sirin lived a life marked by humility. Historical accounts describe him as deeply scrupulous in his personal conduct — he reportedly spent years repaying a debt he believed might have been incurred improperly. This commitment to ethical precision carried over directly into his approach to dreams: interpretation, he insisted, was a grave responsibility not to be taken lightly.

"Dream interpretation is one-seventieth of prophecy. Let no one interpret dreams except one who knows and is righteous — for the interpretation falls upon the dreamer."
— Attributed to Ibn Sirin

The Ibn Sirin Methodology

What distinguished Ibn Sirin from previous dream interpreters — including the ancient Egyptians and Greeks — was his systematic, principled methodology. Rather than relying on fixed symbol-to-meaning tables, he developed a contextual framework that considered multiple factors before reaching any interpretation.

01

Who is the Dreamer?

The same symbol means different things for a king, a scholar, a farmer, or a traveller. Context of the person is paramount.

02

Time of the Dream

Dreams at the end of the night (near dawn) are considered more likely to be truthful than those in deep sleep.

03

Emotional State

The dreamer's fear, joy, or peace within the dream carries interpretive weight. Feelings are not incidental.

04

Current Circumstances

A sick person dreaming of water may mean recovery. A wealthy person dreaming of the same may mean generosity.

05

Quranic & Hadith Roots

Symbols are first interpreted through Quranic meanings. The Quran calls Joseph's sun and moon "parents" — so celestial bodies often mean authority figures.

06

Arabic Language

Word roots and puns matter. Dreaming of a ward (rose) may relate to a ward (arrival of news). Language is a dream tool.

Vision vs. Dream: A Critical Distinction

One of Ibn Sirin's most important contributions was clearly defining the difference between a ru'ya (vision) and a hulm (dream). This distinction, which appears in hadith literature, shapes the entire interpretive framework:

Aspect Ru'ya (رؤيا) — Vision Hulm (حلم) — Dream
OriginFrom AllahFrom Shaytan or the self
NatureBrings joy, peace, clarityMay bring fear, disturbance
ActionMay be interpreted and sharedShould not be shared; spit left three times upon waking
TimingMost often near dawn (Fajr)Can occur at any time

ReliabilityPart of the 46 parts of prophecyNot reliable for guidance

Key Symbols in Ibn Sirin's System

While Ibn Sirin resisted rigid tables, scholars have extracted consistent symbol interpretations from his recorded interpretations. Here are some of the most significant:

Water (ماء)

Water is among the most complex symbols. Clear, flowing water generally indicates knowledge, life, and blessings — especially if the dreamer drinks it. Turbid or bitter water may indicate trials or conflict. Flooding water can represent powerful authority or overwhelming circumstance. Crucially, the dreamer's relationship with the water (drowning, swimming, drinking) modifies the meaning dramatically.

Flight (الطيران)

Flying dreams are interpreted based on how the dreamer flies. Soaring effortlessly like a bird indicates elevation in status and dignity. Flying with difficulty or fear may suggest a journey with hardship. Flying toward the sky can indicate nearness to Allah or death approaching — depending on context.

Teeth (الأسنان)

Teeth represent family members — the upper teeth are males, lower teeth females, the front teeth are close family. A falling tooth can indicate the death or departure of the corresponding relative. New teeth growing may indicate the birth of a new family member or a new addition to the household.

Famous Interpretations

The Man Who Dreamed of Honey

A man came to Ibn Sirin saying he had dreamed of eating honey. Ibn Sirin asked: "Are you currently memorising the Quran?" The man said yes. Ibn Sirin replied: "Then continue — you will find sweetness in it." The honey symbolised the sweetness of Quranic knowledge entering the heart — but only because of the dreamer's specific context.

The Iron Lock

Another account tells of two men who came to Ibn Sirin with the same dream — they had each seen their mouth locked with an iron lock. For the first man, who was known for falsehood and gossip, Ibn Sirin interpreted it as a warning: he would be prevented from continuing his harmful speech. For the second man, known for fasting and worship, he said: "You will continue fasting until you break it with the truth." Same symbol, radically different meanings — the essence of his methodology.

Al-Nabulsi: Carrying the Tradition Forward

Abd al-Ghani al-Nabulsi (1641–1731 CE), a Syrian Sufi scholar, built upon Ibn Sirin's foundation in his monumental work Ta'tir al-Anam fi Tafsir al-Ahlam (Perfuming Humanity in Dream Interpretation). Running to several volumes and covering thousands of symbols, it remains the most comprehensive Islamic dream dictionary ever compiled.

Al-Nabulsi maintained Ibn Sirin's contextual approach while systematising the symbol library further. He also introduced more explicit connections between Sufi spiritual states and dream content — reflecting the mystical tradition he represented.

A Legacy That Reaches the Present

The methodology Ibn Sirin established — contextual, person-centred, linguistically sensitive, rooted in scripture — is remarkably aligned with modern psychological approaches to dream analysis. Both Carl Jung and Sigmund Freud, working centuries later, insisted that the same symbol means different things to different people. Ibn Sirin established this principle in the 7th century CE.
